How does it work?

For doing citizen science we need two important components, a Platform and a project.

  • Platforms

    • Platforms are websites, applications or even online forms which can be used to collect and organise data from volunteers. iNaturalist, eBird, google forms, kobo collect and social media platforms etc. are examples of freely available platforms to collect data. Some platforms let you create your own project of interest, some other platforms invite you to contribute to the projects they have already initiated.

Some of the most popular platforms are listed here. You can check out the training page to learn more about how to use each of these platforms.

  • Social media

    Social media is a great opportunity to collect data from the masses. There are many social media groups such as “Bird watchers of Kerala'' aimed at sharing biodiversity information. You can create a page and invite others to share the data of your interest or share online forms through social media

  • eBIRD

    eBird is a project of the Cornell lab of ornithology co founded by the Audobon society. ‘Bird count India’ handle the India specific platform. eBird doesn't lets you create your own projects, You can contribute to their projects such as the Great backyard bird count, Bird atlas etc. or just submit your locality specific data on your own. The collected data can be accessed publicly.
    eBird India:https://ebird.org/india/home

  • iNaturalist

    Here you can create your own projects. You can invite people to join the project by sharing the URL or keep it open for all. Also anyone can access area specific or taxa specific data easily. More than 5.5 million users are contributing to iNaturalist and have recorded more than hundred million observations so far.
    iNaturalist: https://www.inaturalist.org

  • KoboToolbox

    The Kobotoolbox is an open source tool for collecting data mainly relating to humanitarian activities with unlimited storage. Currently the platform also provides a toolbox for researchers. Its versatile features such as drop down menu, skip logic and validation makes it one of the most effective data collection tools available for free.

  • Google form

    Google form can be a very versatile way of collecting data. Owned by the tech giant Google, google forms are free to use for personal needs and can be stored using our own personal cloud storage. No app is required at both the end, sender and receiver. Google forms are not purpose built for collecting biodiversity data. But there are several options to customise it accordingly.

There are many other platforms such as Biodiversity atlas, Butterflies of India etc. These platforms don't let you create your own project, instead these act as a project itself. We can contribute biodiversity data to these projects, also anyone can download the desired data. These platforms/projects store their own recordings and act as a repository also. So it's hard to differentiate these platforms based on their purpose as platforms, projects or repositories. Details of which are discussed in the Projects page.
